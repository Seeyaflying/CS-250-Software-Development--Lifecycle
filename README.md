# CS-250-Software-Development--Lifecycle

Questions:

How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating “user stories” help with this?

How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?

What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?



Response:

When trying to understand what a user really wants, listen to them and ask why they want something. Don’t just accept their words at face value. When you learn what a user needs, try to write it down in a sentence. It should follow this format: As a user, I want to do something so I can get something. This sentence shows who will benefit, what they need, and why it matters. Next, add a few conditions that explain when the feature is complete. 



Each story is small and focused, allowing the team to estimate the amount of work required, prioritize the most important tasks, and continue discussing what truly adds value to the user. At the start of a project, begin with a discovery phase. In this phase, talk to stakeholders, write a few user stories, and rank them by importance. When you plan a sprint, choose the user stories that the team believes can be completed in the next two to four weeks. Agree on what it means for each story to be done, and add them to the sprint backlog. 



Before coding, draw a design or conduct short research if something is unclear. Don't overthink it; just provide guidance for implementation. Then code in steps, saving your work often to a system that runs automated tests. At the end of the sprint, present a demo to stakeholders, gather their feedback, and discuss what went well and what didn’t. 



When the sprint is stable, release it to a staging environment, run some checks, and then push it to production. This process allows you to gather input for the next set of user stories. Several practices work well with this flow, such as fixed-length sprints, regular meetings, Kanban boards, limits on work in progress, test-driven development, and CI/CD pipelines that automate building, testing, and deploying. You don’t have to use all these techniques at once. Choose the ones that are most helpful and add more over time. 



Being a team member is not about your title; it’s about your actions. Communicate clearly, share updates, mention any problems, and ask questions if you’re unsure about something. Respect the agreed-upon process, attend meetings, and follow through on what you committed to during the sprint. Collaborate with your team by helping each other with difficult problems, reviewing each other’s code, and sharing knowledge. Ensure quality by writing tests, fixing bugs, and avoiding shortcuts that could lead to future issues. Be ready to adjust to changes and welcome feedback. Be willing to modify your plans as needed. 



Finally, contribute to a positive team culture by celebrating achievements, recognizing hard work, and keeping discussions focused on finding solutions. In short, take what the user needs and turn it into small stories. Follow an agile process of planning, building, reviewing, and adjusting. Use practices that make your work easier. Collaborate with your team. This approach will help you create software and benefit you in any software development class.
